## 1. A definition that can grow with you

**Electricity is a general name for phenomena associated with electric charge and electric current.** We describe those phenomena using electric and magnetic fields, forces, material behavior, and energy transfer.

Electricity is therefore not one substance that a source pours into a wire. Electric charge is a property of matter. A distribution of charge produces an electric field, and redistributing charge changes that field. In a complete system, fields exert forces on charged particles and help transfer energy from a source to a load.

<aside className="callout">

**First mental model:** matter already contains charged particles. A source establishes an electrical condition; fields and materials determine how local charge carriers respond; energy changes form in the load.

</aside>

## 2. Matter already contains charge

Atoms contain positively charged protons and negatively charged electrons. An everyday object described as neutral has nearly equal total positive and negative charge, so its **net charge** is near zero. Neutral does not mean “charge-free”: the object can still become polarized, and local electric fields can still exist.

Charge is conserved: it can move between objects or regions, but an isolated system does not create or destroy net charge. For ordinary observable charge, the amount appears in integer multiples of the elementary charge.

Different materials provide different mobile charge carriers:

- **Metals** such as copper conduct mainly through mobile electrons.
- **Insulators** bind charge more strongly, although their behavior still depends on field strength, geometry, temperature, frequency, and breakdown limits.
- **Semiconductors** use controllable electron and hole populations; material structure, doping, fields, temperature, and light can all matter.
- **Electrolytes** conduct mainly through moving ions, while **plasmas** contain mobile electrons and ions.

Current direction is defined by convention as the direction positive charge would move. In a copper wire, the average electron drift points in the opposite direction.

</section>

<section>

## 3. Field, potential difference, and a closed path

An electric field describes the electric force per unit positive test charge at a location. Charges have fields even before a conducting loop is completed. Moving or redistributing charge changes the field pattern; fields do not suddenly begin to exist only when a switch closes.

**Voltage is electric potential difference between two points.** In the electrostatic model, it tells us the change in electric potential energy per unit charge between those points. A battery uses electrochemical reactions to do work on charge and maintain a terminal potential difference while remaining nearly neutral overall.

With a switch open, a battery can still have terminal voltage and electric fields, including a field across the contact gap. The broken path prevents a **sustained loop current**. When the contacts meet, the circuit's electromagnetic boundary conditions change. That disturbance propagates through the physical conductors and return path in finite time; its speed depends on geometry and materials. Local carriers respond after the change reaches them, while their average drift remains much slower.

</section>

<section>

## 4. Keep these quantities separate

- **Voltage `V` — volt (`V = J/C`):** electric potential difference between two points.
- **Current `I` — ampere (`A = C/s`):** rate at which charge crosses a chosen surface, `I = dQ/dt`.
- **Resistance `R` — ohm (`Ω = V/A`):** ratio `V/I` for a resistive two-terminal element at a stated operating condition.
- **Power `P` — watt (`W = J/s`):** rate of energy transfer; for steady DC, `P = VI`.
- **Energy — joule (`J`):** amount transferred or converted over time, `energy = ∫ P dt`.

For an approximately ohmic element over a stated range, `V = IR`. That does not make resistance a universal constant. Temperature, material, geometry, frequency, and operating point can change the relationship.

</section>

## 5. How a battery lights an incandescent lamp

1. Electrochemical reactions in the battery maintain a terminal potential difference.
2. With the switch open, fields and terminal voltage can exist, but the broken path prevents sustained loop current.
3. When the contacts meet, the electromagnetic boundary conditions change.
4. The change propagates through the circuit. Reflections and other transients occur before the circuit approaches a steady operating state.
5. Local electric fields in the conductors drive charge carriers. Conventional current points from the source's positive terminal through the external circuit; copper-electron drift points the other way.
6. In the filament, electrical energy is converted mostly into thermal energy. As the filament becomes hot, it emits a broad spectrum containing infrared and visible light.
7. In steady DC, the electrical power received by the lamp can be calculated as `P = VI` at its terminals.

## 6. What the simple lamp model leaves out

A real incandescent lamp is not a fixed 30 Ω resistor during turn-on. A cold filament normally has lower resistance, so its initial current can be higher. Its resistance rises as it heats, and its light output follows a slower thermal process. A real battery also has internal resistance, and wires and switch contacts add resistance and inductance.

These omitted effects are not errors when the boundary is visible. The simple model answers one narrow question: **what differs between an open circuit and the final idealized DC condition?** Separate lessons can later model signal propagation, contact bounce and arcing, filament warm-up, source voltage sag, and thermal radiation without mixing their time scales.

</section>

## 7. The first branches of the electricity map

- **Static electricity** focuses on charge imbalance, polarization, fields, force, and discharge.
- **Direct current (DC)** describes current whose conventional direction does not reverse; its magnitude may still vary.
- **Alternating current (AC)** changes direction periodically and is described with waveform, frequency, phase, impedance, and power concepts.
- **Magnetism** is inseparable from moving charge and current at deeper levels.
- **Electromagnetic induction** connects changing magnetic flux with induced electric effects and is central to transformers, generators, motors, and many sensors.
- **Electronics** controls charge and fields in materials and devices such as diodes, transistors, sensors, processors, and power converters.

The atlas treats these as connected branches, not isolated definitions.

</section>

## 8. Common traps

- **“A neutral object contains no charge.”** Its positive and negative charges approximately balance; they have not disappeared.
- **“Voltage flows.”** Charge can cross a surface; voltage compares electric potential between points.
- **“Current is used up.”** In a steady lumped single-path circuit with negligible charge accumulation, the same current passes each series element. Energy is transferred and converted.
- **“Electrons race around the whole loop at light speed.”** A change in electromagnetic state propagates through the circuit much faster than the average electron drift.
- **“All circuit energy travels inside the copper.”** Electromagnetic power flow is a field-and-system question. The wire guides the circuit behavior, but the complete energy story involves fields in and around conductors and components.
- **“Ground means zero volts and safe.”** Ground can mean a circuit reference, chassis, system ground, reference Earth, or protective conductor. These are not automatically interchangeable, and none makes arbitrary contact safe.

</section>

## Technical track: equations and their limits

For ordinary observable charge, `q = N e`, where `q` is charge in coulombs (`C`), `N` is a signed integer, and the elementary-charge magnitude is exactly `e = 1.602 176 634 × 10⁻¹⁹ C` in SI.

The electric force on a stationary charge is `𝐅ₑ = q𝐄`. The full electromagnetic force on a point charge is `𝐅 = q(𝐄 + 𝐯 × 𝐁)`, where `𝐅` is force in newtons (`N`), `𝐄` is electric-field strength in volts per metre (`V/m`), `𝐯` is velocity in metres per second (`m/s`), and `𝐁` is magnetic flux density in teslas (`T`).

In electrostatic or other conservative-field conditions, `ΔV = ΔU/q` and `𝐄 = −∇V`. Here `ΔV` is potential difference in volts, `ΔU` is electric-potential-energy change in joules, and `∇V` is the spatial gradient of potential. A time-varying magnetic field can produce a nonconservative electric field, so one global scalar-potential story is not sufficient for every electromagnetic situation.

Current is `I = dQ/dt`. In a local, linear, isotropic ohmic material, the constitutive model is `𝐉 = σ𝐄`, where current density `𝐉` is in amperes per square metre (`A/m²`) and conductivity `σ` is in siemens per metre (`S/m`). This is a material model, not a universal law. Local charge conservation is `∇·𝐉 + ∂ρ/∂t = 0`, where `ρ` is volume charge density in coulombs per cubic metre (`C/m³`) and `∇·` denotes divergence.

Instantaneous terminal power is `p(t) = u(t)i(t)`; the steady-DC form is `P = VI`. Electromagnetic power-flow density is described by the Poynting vector `𝐒 = 𝐄 × 𝐇` in watts per square metre (`W/m²`), where magnetic-field strength `𝐇` is in amperes per metre (`A/m`). This field description complements circuit theory; it does not justify saying energy exists only inside or only outside a conductor.

Maxwell's equations connect charge, current, electric fields, magnetic fields, and changing fields. Constitutive relations such as `𝐉 = σ𝐄` then describe how particular materials respond.

</section>

## 9. Safety and Thailand scope

This page is a conceptual explanation, not a wiring or live-work procedure. Do not reproduce the drawing with household mains, and do not short-circuit a battery. Real sources and lamps require correctly rated components; conductors, contacts, and filaments can become hot enough to burn or start a fire.

The lesson makes no claim about an exact Thai installation rule or legal threshold. When the atlas adds Thai wiring, protection, utility, or public-safety instructions, the exact current source, edition, scope, and applicable authority must be verified before publication.
