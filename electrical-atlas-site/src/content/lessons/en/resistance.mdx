## 1. A useful definition

**Resistance** is how strongly a material, component, or path opposes electric current.

The unit is the **ohm**, written `Ω`.

**Conductance** is the opposite idea: how easily a path lets current through.

The unit is the **siemens**, written `S`.

The two are reciprocals:

`G = 1 / R`

`R = 1 / G`

In plain language: resistance is electrical “hard to pass through”; conductance is electrical “easy to pass through.”

<aside className="callout">

**First mental model:** resistance is like a narrow or rough path for charge movement. A wider, smoother path has more conductance. This is only a model, but it helps explain why the same voltage can create different currents.

</aside>

</section>

<section>

## 2. Why resistance matters

Resistance is the missing piece that connects voltage to current.

If voltage is the energy difference per charge, and current is how much charge crosses a point per second, resistance tells us how much current the circuit path allows for a given voltage.

That leads to the most famous beginner circuit relationship:

`I = V / R`

Resistance matters because it controls:

- LED current,
- heating in wires and PCB traces,
- voltage drops,
- sensor readings,
- battery drain,
- signal loading,
- motor and coil current,
- short-circuit severity,
- power loss and efficiency.

</section>

## 3. Where you see resistance in real life

You see resistance in:

- resistors,
- wires and cable runs,
- contacts and connectors,
- switches and relays,
- heating elements,
- incandescent lamp filaments,
- motor windings,
- PCB traces,
- sensors such as thermistors and strain gauges,
- semiconductor channels such as MOSFET on-resistance.

Sometimes resistance is intentional, like a resistor that limits LED current. Sometimes it is unwanted, like contact resistance that makes a connector heat up.

</section>

<section>

## 4. What is involved

Resistance depends on several things:

- **Material**: copper conducts well; rubber insulates well; carbon, silicon, and alloys can sit between extremes.
- **Length**: a longer path usually has more resistance.
- **Cross-sectional area**: a wider path usually has less resistance.
- **Temperature**: many metals increase resistance as they heat; some materials do the opposite.
- **Geometry**: bends, thin traces, constrictions, and contacts matter.
- **Device physics**: semiconductors, lamps, thermistors, and arcs are not always simple fixed resistors.

For a uniform conductor, a common model is:

`R = ρL / A`

where `ρ` is resistivity, `L` is length, and `A` is cross-sectional area.

</section>

<section>

## 5. How resistance works physically

In a metal, mobile electrons respond to the electric field, but they constantly interact with the material lattice, impurities, defects, and vibrations. These interactions scatter the charge carriers and limit the average current.

Step by step:

1. A voltage creates an electric field in a conducting path.
2. Mobile charge carriers respond to that field.
3. Material structure and temperature interrupt the motion through scattering.
4. The result is a limited current for the applied voltage.
5. The electrical energy lost in that path usually becomes heat.

Resistance is not a magic “current eater.” It is a property of the path that converts some electrical energy into heat while setting the current-voltage relationship.

</section>

<section>

## 6. Resistance versus conductance

Resistance and conductance describe the same thing from opposite directions.

- High resistance means low conductance.
- Low resistance means high conductance.
- `1 Ω` corresponds to `1 S`.
- `100 Ω` corresponds to `0.01 S`.
- `1 kΩ` corresponds to `0.001 S`, or `1 mS`.

Most beginner electronics talks about resistance because resistors are labeled in ohms. Conductance becomes useful when thinking about parallel paths, admittance, semiconductor channels, and how “open” a path is.

</section>

<section>

## 7. Main symbols and equations

Common symbols:

- `R`: resistance in ohms.
- `G`: conductance in siemens.
- `V`: voltage in volts.
- `I`: current in amperes.
- `P`: power in watts.

Core equations:

`I = V / R`

`V = IR`

`R = V / I`

`G = 1 / R`

`I = GV`

Power converted to heat in a resistor can be written:

`P = VI`

`P = I²R`

`P = V² / R`

The last equation is why lowering resistance at the same voltage can make heat rise fast.

</section>

## 8. Practical use

Engineers and technicians use resistance to ask:

- What resistor limits this LED to a safe current?
- How much voltage will drop across this wire?
- Will this trace or cable overheat?
- Is this fuse seeing a short-circuit condition?
- Is this sensor resistance changing with temperature, strain, light, or position?
- Is this MOSFET on-resistance low enough for the load current?
- Is this connector developing dangerous contact resistance?

Resistance is usually measured with an ohmmeter when the circuit is unpowered. Measuring resistance inside a powered circuit can damage the meter, damage the circuit, or give meaningless results.

</section>

<section>

## 9. Common mistakes

- **"Resistance uses up current."** Loads convert energy; current is not a consumable fluid.
- **"Resistance is always bad."** Resistance is useful for current limiting, sensing, biasing, heating, damping, and protection.
- **"A resistor always has the same value."** Real parts have tolerance, temperature coefficient, voltage coefficient, aging, noise, and power limits.
- **"Zero ohms means no problem."** Very low resistance can mean huge current if the source can supply it.
- **"An open circuit has high resistance, so it is safe."** It may still have dangerous voltage or stored energy.
- **"Conductance is a different phenomenon."** Conductance is the reciprocal view of resistance.

</section>

<section>

## 10. Failure modes and limits

Resistance-related failures include:

- overheated wires,
- burned resistors,
- PCB trace damage,
- connector heating from contact resistance,
- thermal runaway in some devices,
- insulation breakdown after overheating,
- poor sensor accuracy from lead resistance,
- unexpected voltage drop in long cables,
- fire risk from short circuits and overcurrent.

Current creates heat in resistive paths. If heat cannot leave fast enough, temperature rises, material properties change, and failure can accelerate.

</section>

## Technical track

For a uniform conductor:

`R = ρL / A`

where `ρ` is resistivity. Conductivity is:

`σ = 1 / ρ`

Conductance for the same simple geometry is:

`G = σA / L`

Microscopic Ohm's law relates current density `J` and electric field `E`:

`J = σE`

For many metals over limited ranges, resistance changes approximately with temperature:

`R(T) = R0[1 + α(T - T0)]`

This linear form is only an approximation. Semiconductors, thermistors, lamps, varistors, arcs, and many electronic devices are nonlinear. Their current-voltage relationship cannot be described by one fixed resistance over all operating points.
