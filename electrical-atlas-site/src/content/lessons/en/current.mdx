## 1. A useful definition

**Electric current** is the rate at which electric charge passes through a surface, wire, device terminal, or chosen point in a circuit.

The unit is the **ampere**, usually shortened to **amp**:

`1 A = 1 C/s`

In plain language: current tells you **how much charge crosses a point each second**.

<aside className="callout">

**First mental model:** current is like a people counter at a doorway. The counter does not ask where each person started or where each person will end up. It counts how many pass through the doorway per second.

</aside>

</section>

<section>

## 2. Why current matters

Current is one of the core quantities because it connects electrical ideas to real effects:

- current through a resistor creates heat,
- current through an LED creates light,
- current through a motor winding creates magnetic force,
- current into a capacitor changes its voltage,
- current into a battery charges or discharges it,
- current through the body can be dangerous.

Voltage is energy per charge. Current is charge per time. Together they lead naturally to power:

`P = VI`

That is why voltage alone is not enough. A `5 V` supply that can deliver `3 A` is very different from a `5 V` signal pin that should only source a few milliamps.

</section>

## 3. Where you see current in real life

You see current ratings on power adapters, fuses, circuit breakers, wires, relays, MOSFETs, motors, LEDs, batteries, chargers, and solar equipment.

Examples:

- A small LED may use `5 mA` to `20 mA`.
- A phone charger may supply a few amps.
- A motor may draw a large startup current.
- A household branch circuit, breaker, cable, and outlet all have current limits.

The exact safe current for wiring or equipment depends on voltage, cable size, insulation, installation method, temperature, protection devices, and local rules. Thailand-specific installation content must be handled with verified Thai standards and authority guidance, not guessed from a beginner lesson.

</section>

## 4. What is involved

Current involves:

- **charge carriers**: electrons in metals, ions in electrolytes, electrons and holes in semiconductors,
- **a path**: a conductor, semiconductor channel, plasma, electrolyte, vacuum tube, or other medium,
- **an electric field**: the field that pushes mobile charge carriers,
- **a chosen cross-section**: the surface where we count charge passing through,
- **time**: current is a rate, so time is part of the definition.

A closed path is usually needed for steady current in ordinary circuits. If a path is open, charges may shift briefly while fields settle, but steady conduction current cannot continue through the open gap.

</section>

<section>

## 5. Conventional current and electron motion

Circuit diagrams usually draw **conventional current** in the direction positive charge would move: from higher potential toward lower potential through a load.

In a metal wire, the mobile carriers are mostly electrons. Electrons are negative, so their average drift is opposite the conventional current direction.

This sounds annoying at first, but it is just a sign convention. Circuit equations work consistently as long as we keep the convention straight.

<aside className="callout">

**Important distinction:** current can be large even though individual electrons drift slowly. The field pattern and energy transfer through a circuit can respond much faster than one electron drifts along a whole wire.

</aside>

</section>

## 6. How current works physically

When a source is connected to a load through conductors, the circuit's electric field changes. Mobile charge carriers inside the materials respond to that field.

Step by step:

1. A voltage source establishes an electric field around the connected circuit.
2. Charge carriers inside conductors and devices feel force from that field.
3. They drift with a small average velocity while also colliding constantly with the material.
4. A chosen cross-section of the wire sees charge passing through over time.
5. That rate of charge crossing the section is the current.

The wire is already full of mobile charge carriers. A battery does not need to "send one electron from the battery to the lamp" before anything happens. It changes the field conditions that organize charge movement throughout the circuit.

</section>

## 7. Main symbols and equations

Common symbols:

- `I`: current, measured in amperes.
- `q` or `Q`: electric charge, measured in coulombs.
- `t`: time, measured in seconds.
- `A`: ampere as a unit, not to be confused with area.

The beginner equation is:

`I = Q / t`

If `3 C` of charge passes a point in `2 s`, the current is:

`I = 3 C / 2 s = 1.5 A`

The reverse form is:

`Q = It`

So a `2 A` current for `5 s` means `10 C` of charge has passed the chosen point.

</section>

## 8. Practical use

Engineers and technicians use current to ask practical questions:

- Will this wire overheat?
- Will this fuse or breaker trip?
- Can this power supply deliver enough current?
- Is the motor startup current too high?
- Is this LED protected by enough resistance or current regulation?
- Is this MOSFET within its current and thermal limits?
- Is this battery charging or discharging safely?

Current is usually measured **through** something, so an ammeter is placed in series with the branch being measured. Clamp meters measure current by sensing the magnetic field around a conductor, which is especially useful for higher-current systems.

</section>

## 9. Common mistakes

- **"Current is the same as voltage."** Voltage is energy per charge; current is charge per time.
- **"Current gets used up."** Energy is converted in loads. In a simple series loop, the same current enters and leaves each element.
- **"Electrons move at near light speed."** The electromagnetic influence is fast; electron drift in a metal wire is usually slow.
- **"A supply pushes its rated current all the time."** A `5 V 2 A` adapter can supply up to about `2 A`; the load determines the actual current within limits.
- **"Only high voltage is dangerous."** Current through the body, path, time, frequency, environment, and available energy all matter.

</section>

<section>

## 10. Failure modes and limits

Too much current can cause:

- overheating wires and PCB traces,
- damaged connectors and contacts,
- blown fuses or tripped breakers,
- burned resistors, LEDs, motors, or ICs,
- MOSFET overcurrent and thermal runaway,
- battery stress, venting, fire, or explosion,
- electric shock hazards when current passes through a person.

Current ratings are not just numbers on a label. They depend on heat, duration, cooling, enclosure, cable installation, ambient temperature, and protection coordination.

</section>

## Technical track

Instantaneous current is:

`I = dq/dt`

For distributed conduction, current density `J` describes current per unit area:

`I = ∫ J · dA`

For a simple conductor with carrier density `n`, carrier charge `q`, cross-sectional area `A`, and average drift velocity `vd`:

`I = nqAvd`

Charge conservation is expressed by the continuity equation:

`∂ρ/∂t + ∇ · J = 0`

In semiconductors, current can include drift and diffusion components. In capacitors, changing electric field creates displacement current. At high frequencies, current is no longer just "flow in one wire"; return paths, fields, skin effect, parasitics, and transmission-line behavior matter.

</section>
