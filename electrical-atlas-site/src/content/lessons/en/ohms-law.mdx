## 1. The useful idea

**Ohm's law** is the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance for an **ohmic** path.

The most common form is:

`V = I × R`

where:

- `V` is voltage in volts,
- `I` is current in amperes,
- `R` is resistance in ohms.

You can rearrange the same idea:

`I = V / R`

`R = V / I`

In plain language: for a fixed resistance, more voltage produces more current. For a fixed voltage, more resistance allows less current.

<aside className="callout">

**First mental model:** voltage is the electrical push, resistance is how difficult the path is, and current is the resulting flow rate.

</aside>

</section>

<section>

## 2. Why Ohm's law matters

Ohm's law is usually the first practical circuit law because it lets you turn one known pair into the missing third value.

You can use it to ask:

- What current flows through a resistor?
- What resistor value limits current to a safe amount?
- What voltage drop appears across a wire or resistor?
- How much current might a short circuit try to draw?
- What power turns into heat in a resistive part?

It is the bridge between the previous foundation lessons:

- voltage: energy per charge,
- current: charge flow per second,
- resistance: how strongly the path opposes current.

</section>

<section>

## 3. The three forms

Use the form that matches the unknown you need.

If current is unknown:

`I = V / R`

If voltage is unknown:

`V = I × R`

If resistance is unknown:

`R = V / I`

Example:

If a `12 V` source is connected across `24 Ω`:

`I = 12 / 24 = 0.5 A`

The current is half an ampere.

</section>

<section>

## 4. Power is the practical warning

Ohm's law tells current, but real parts also heat up.

Electrical power is:

`P = V × I`

For a resistor, you can combine this with Ohm's law:

`P = I²R`

`P = V² / R`

That last form is important: at the same voltage, lowering resistance can make power rise very quickly.

A resistor, wire, connector, PCB trace, MOSFET, fuse, or battery path can fail if the heat cannot escape.

</section>

<section>

## 5. When Ohm's law works well

Ohm's law works well when the path behaves like a mostly fixed resistance over the operating range.

Good beginner examples:

- ordinary resistors within their rating,
- short pieces of wire at low current,
- many metal conductors over a small temperature range,
- a MOSFET on-resistance as a rough local approximation,
- current-sense resistors,
- heating elements when modeled over a limited range.

In those cases, the graph of current versus voltage is close to a straight line.

</section>

<section>

## 6. When Ohm's law is not enough

Many real devices are **non-ohmic**. Their current-voltage relationship is not one fixed straight line.

Examples:

- LEDs and diodes,
- MOSFETs outside the simple on-resistance region,
- incandescent lamps as the filament heats up,
- motors during startup,
- batteries under load,
- thermistors and varistors,
- arcs, sparks, and breakdown regions.

You can sometimes use a local or approximate resistance, but the simple formula may mislead you if the device changes with voltage, current, temperature, light, magnetic field, or time.

</section>

<section>

## 7. A beginner circuit example

Suppose you have a `9 V` battery and want about `20 mA` through an LED path.

Ignoring the LED for a moment would give:

`R = V / I = 9 / 0.02 = 450 Ω`

But an LED is not an ordinary resistor. It has a forward voltage. If the LED drops about `2 V`, the resistor sees about:

`9 V - 2 V = 7 V`

Then:

`R = 7 / 0.02 = 350 Ω`

This is why Ohm's law is powerful, but only after you know which part of the circuit is actually acting like the resistor.

</section>

<section>

## 8. Common mistakes

- **Using Ohm's law on the wrong part of the circuit.** Apply it to a specific element or path, not vaguely to "everything."
- **Forgetting units.** Use volts, amperes, and ohms together.
- **Treating LEDs as resistors.** LEDs need a current-limiting element or driver.
- **Ignoring power.** A mathematically correct resistor value can still burn if the wattage is too low.
- **Assuming resistance never changes.** Temperature, tolerance, aging, and device physics matter.
- **Confusing a short circuit with "low resistance is good."** Low resistance at a strong source can mean dangerous current.

</section>

<section>

## 9. Safety notes

Ohm's law is central to safety, but it is not a replacement for electrical design rules.

Do not use this lesson alone to size building wires, choose breakers, work on mains wiring, repair appliances, design battery packs, or modify switchboards.

For Thailand-specific installation or safety content, verify current TISI/TIS, EIT, MEA, PEA, ERC, and related IEC/IEEE references before publication. The formula is stable; the legal and installation rules must be checked from official sources.

</section>

## Technical track

Ohm's law has several related forms.

For a circuit element:

`v = Ri`

For conductance:

`i = Gv`

For a material in a local field approximation:

`J = σE`

where `J` is current density, `σ` is conductivity, and `E` is electric field.

Real devices may need differential or small-signal resistance:

`r = dV / dI`

That is different from the simple large-signal ratio `R = V / I` when the device is nonlinear.
