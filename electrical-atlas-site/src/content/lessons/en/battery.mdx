## 1. The useful idea

A **battery** is an electrochemical energy source.

It stores energy in chemicals, then uses chemical reactions to maintain a voltage between two terminals.

When you connect a circuit between the positive and negative terminals:

- the battery creates an electric field through the circuit,
- charge carriers in the circuit start moving,
- current flows through the load,
- chemical energy inside the battery is converted into electrical energy and then into heat, light, motion, sound, or stored energy somewhere else.

<aside className="callout">

**First mental model:** a battery is not a box full of loose electrons. It is a chemical pump that keeps the two terminals at different electrical potentials.

</aside>

</section>

<section>

## 2. Cell, battery, and pack

People often say “battery” for everything, but the words can be more precise:

- A **cell** is one electrochemical unit.
- A **battery** is one or more cells connected together.
- A **battery pack** adds structure, wiring, protection, sensing, balancing, thermal design, and sometimes communication.

Examples:

- a single alkaline AA cell is one cell,
- a 9 V rectangular battery contains several small cells,
- a power-bank or EV battery is a pack made from many cells plus protection and control electronics.

</section>

<section>

## 3. Where the voltage comes from

Inside a battery, two different electrode materials are separated by an electrolyte.

The chemistry prefers electrons to be at different energy levels on the two sides. That difference appears at the terminals as voltage.

When the circuit is open, the battery can hold a voltage with almost no external current.

When the circuit is closed, chemical reactions can keep separating charge while the external circuit lets energy leave the battery.

This is why a battery can push current through a lamp, resistor, microcontroller, motor, or phone.

</section>

<section>

## 4. How a battery lights a load

A simple battery-load circuit needs a closed path.

When the path is closed:

1. the battery sets up voltage between its terminals,
2. the electric field appears through the connected circuit very quickly,
3. current flows according to the load and source limits,
4. power moves from the battery to the load,
5. the battery's stored chemical energy decreases.

For a simple resistive load, Ohm's law can estimate current:

`I = V / R`

Power delivered to the load is:

`P = V × I`

Energy used over time is:

`E = P × t`

</section>

<section>

## 5. Capacity: amp-hours and watt-hours

Battery capacity is often written in **amp-hours**:

`Ah = current × time`

For example, a `2 Ah` cell can ideally provide `2 A` for `1 h`, or `1 A` for `2 h`.

But amp-hours are not total energy by themselves. Energy also needs voltage:

`Wh = V × Ah`

So a `2 Ah` cell at `3.7 V` is roughly:

`3.7 × 2 = 7.4 Wh`

while a `2 Ah` battery at `12 V` is roughly:

`12 × 2 = 24 Wh`

Same amp-hours, different energy.

</section>

<section>

## 6. Internal resistance and voltage sag

Real batteries are not ideal voltage sources.

They have internal resistance and other internal limits. Under load, the terminal voltage drops:

`V_terminal ≈ V_open - I × R_internal`

This drop is called **voltage sag**.

Internal resistance also makes heat:

`P_heat = I²R_internal`

This is why a weak, cold, old, small, or damaged battery may show a reasonable voltage with no load but collapse when a real load is connected.

</section>

<section>

## 7. C-rate

**C-rate** compares load current to battery capacity.

`C-rate = current / capacity`

For a `2 Ah` cell:

- `1 A` is `0.5 C`,
- `2 A` is `1 C`,
- `4 A` is `2 C`.

Higher C-rate means the battery is being charged or discharged harder.

Different chemistries and cell designs allow different C-rates. Do not assume a battery can safely deliver any current just because the voltage and capacity look large enough.

</section>

<section>

## 8. Series and parallel cells

Cells are combined to make useful battery voltages and capacities.

**Series connection** increases voltage:

- two `3.7 V` cells in series are roughly `7.4 V`,
- three are roughly `11.1 V`.

**Parallel connection** increases capacity and current capability:

- two `2 Ah` cells in parallel are roughly `4 Ah` at the same voltage.

Series and parallel packs need protection and balance. One weak cell can limit or endanger the whole pack.

</section>

<section>

## 9. Charging is chemistry-specific

Charging is not just “push current backward.”

Different batteries need different charging rules:

- lithium-ion cells usually need controlled current, controlled voltage, temperature limits, and protection,
- lead-acid batteries use different voltage stages and behavior,
- nickel-based batteries have their own termination methods,
- primary cells are generally not rechargeable.

Using the wrong charger can overheat, leak, vent, damage, or ignite a battery.

</section>

<section>

## 10. Common mistakes

- **Thinking voltage alone tells remaining energy.** Voltage helps, but chemistry, load, temperature, and history matter.
- **Comparing batteries only by amp-hours.** Watt-hours are better for energy comparison.
- **Ignoring internal resistance.** Voltage sag and heating may dominate under load.
- **Ignoring C-rate.** Capacity does not automatically mean high-current capability.
- **Mixing cells casually.** Different age, charge, chemistry, capacity, or condition can create unsafe imbalance.
- **Treating all batteries like lithium-ion.** Alkaline, lead-acid, NiMH, lithium-ion, LFP, and other chemistries behave differently.
- **Using an unknown charger.** Charging rules are part of the battery system, not an optional detail.

</section>

<section>

## 11. Safety notes

Batteries can store a lot of energy in a small volume.

Possible hazards include short-circuit current, burns, fire, toxic or corrosive materials, vented gas, swelling, thermal runaway, arcing, explosion of sealed cells, and environmental disposal problems.

Do not use this lesson alone to design packs, choose protection, repair damaged batteries, charge unknown cells, work on EV batteries, modify power banks, or connect batteries to building wiring.

For Thailand-specific product, installation, transport, recycling, or safety content, verify current TISI/TIS, EIT, MEA, PEA, ERC, Department of Industrial Works, transport authority, and related IEC/IEEE/UN references before publication. The physics ideas are stable; compliance details must be checked from current official sources.

</section>

## Technical track

A simple battery model is an ideal voltage source plus internal resistance:

`V_terminal = V_oc - I × R_internal`

Load power is:

`P_load = V_terminal × I`

Internal heating is:

`P_loss = I²R_internal`

Remaining energy can be approximated from:

`Wh ≈ V_nominal × Ah_remaining`

This is only a first model.

Real battery voltage depends on state of charge, temperature, aging, rest time, current direction, diffusion, electrochemical kinetics, hysteresis, cell construction, and protection circuitry.

For deeper work, battery models may include equivalent circuits, lookup tables, coulomb counting, Kalman filters, electrochemical models, thermal models, and aging models.

</section>
