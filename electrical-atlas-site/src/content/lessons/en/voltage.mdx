## 1. A useful definition

**Voltage**, or **electric potential difference**, is the change in electric potential energy per unit charge between two points.

The unit is the **volt**. One volt means **one joule per coulomb**:

`1 V = 1 J/C`

In plain language: voltage tells you how much the electric potential energy per unit charge changes from one point to another.

<aside className="callout">

**First mental model:** electric potential is like height, and voltage is like the height difference between two places. This comparison follows the convention of a **positive test charge**. An electron is negatively charged, so the sign of its potential-energy change is reversed for the same voltage change.

</aside>

</section>

<section>

## 2. Why voltage matters

Voltage appears on sources, components, signals, and warning labels:

- An alkaline AA cell may be labelled `1.5 V`; its operating voltage changes with load and condition.
- Vehicle batteries and power adapters have nominal voltage labels, but their exact terminal voltage can vary.
- Logic pins have allowed voltage ranges that must match the device datasheet.
- Capacitors, cables, switches, and insulation have voltage limits.

Voltage is not the whole safety or performance story. A static-electric source may reach a high voltage while storing little energy. A source with substantial stored energy and available current can cause severe short-circuit heating, burns, or fire. Shock and thermal/fire risks depend on the whole source, path, contact, environment, and exposure—not on one number alone.

<aside className="callout">

**Thailand context:** the [Energy Regulatory Commission residential tariff page](https://www.erc.or.th/th/tariff/1288) describes some residential meter categories as `230 V`, single-phase, two-wire service. That is a scoped tariff/service example, not a universal installation rule. Exact service conditions must be checked with the current authority and serving utility.

</aside>

</section>

<section>

## 3. Where you see voltage

You see voltage on batteries, adapters, solar panels, chargers, multimeters, motor drivers, logic pins, capacitors, and hazard labels.

When someone says “this point is 5 volts,” the hidden question is: **5 volts relative to what?** The reference might be a circuit ground or reference node, a supply terminal, a chassis, or another named point.

These references are not automatically interchangeable. Circuit ground does not necessarily connect to Earth, and neither “ground” nor chassis is guaranteed to be safe in every system.

Voltage is always a comparison between two points.

</section>

<section>

## 4. What is involved

Voltage involves:

- **Two points**: a potential difference compares them.
- **Electric charge**: a physical property and quantity carried by particles and objects.
- **Electric potential energy**: energy associated with charge and position in an electric field.
- **Electric field**: a field that can exert force on charge; it can exist without a closed conducting path.
- **Reference point**: the point assigned a convenient potential, often `0 V`, for analysis or measurement.

A battery uses chemistry to maintain a potential difference between its terminals, including when the external circuit is open. Closing a conducting path allows sustained carrier motion and energy transfer through a load.

</section>

<section>

## 5. How voltage works physically

Imagine a battery maintaining separated charge internally. Its positive terminal is at a higher electric potential than its negative terminal.

When a load and a closed conducting path connect the terminals:

1. The source maintains a potential difference.
2. Connecting the circuit establishes an operating electric-field pattern in and around it.
3. Mobile charge carriers respond to the field.
4. Electromagnetic fields mediate energy transfer from the source toward the load.
5. The load changes that energy into heat, light, motion, sound, stored energy, or the energy used to process and signal information.

Mobile charge carriers are already present throughout a metal conductor. They respond to the circuit's electric field; the load does not wait for one particular electron to travel all the way from the battery.

</section>

<section>

## 6. Main symbols and equations

Common symbols:

- `V_A`, `V_B`: electric potential at points A and B, measured in volts.
- `ΔV`: the change in electric potential when going from A to B, measured in volts and defined here as `V_B - V_A`.
- `q`: electric charge, measured in coulombs.
- `ΔU`: change in electric potential energy, measured in joules.

The signed relationships are:

`ΔV = V_B - V_A = ΔU / q`

`ΔU = qΔV`

Here, “from A to B” means **final minus initial**, so `ΔV = V_B - V_A`. The two-subscript notation `V_AB`, meaning “A relative to B,” uses `V_A - V_B` instead. These conventions therefore have opposite signs; always check how the notation is defined.

If a charge with magnitude `1 C` crosses a potential difference with magnitude `9 V`, the **magnitude** of its potential-energy change is `9 J`. The sign depends on the charge sign and the chosen direction. A positive test charge moved from lower to higher potential gains potential energy; an electron behaves oppositely for the same movement.

<aside className="callout">

**Simplification warning:** real sources have internal impedance, current and energy limits, temperature effects, and—in batteries—chemistry. A voltage label alone does not tell you the available power, stored energy, or safety level.

</aside>

## 7. Practical use and measurement

Engineers and technicians use voltage to ask questions such as:

- Is this supply within the circuit's allowed range?
- Is this logic level valid for the chip?
- Is this component's voltage rating sufficient?
- How much does a source's terminal voltage change under load?
- Is this circuit point at the expected potential relative to its named reference?

A voltmeter compares **two points**. Conceptually, it is connected in parallel across the component or points being compared, and a real meter affects the circuit slightly because its input resistance is finite.

<aside className="callout safety-callout">

**Measurement boundary:** this connection fact is not an instruction to probe household mains or another high-energy circuit. Safe work on such circuits depends on task-specific training, procedures, and controls. Do not use this lesson as a live-measurement guide.

</aside>

</section>

<section>

## 8. Common mistakes

- **“Voltage flows.”** Current describes charge flow rate. Voltage is a difference between two points.
- **“A point has voltage by itself.”** A quoted voltage always uses an explicit or implied reference.
- **“One voltage number tells me the danger.”** It does not. Shock and thermal/fire hazards involve different mechanisms and depend on the complete situation.
- **“Battery voltage is battery capacity.”** Voltage is not capacity or stored energy. Capacity and energy need other quantities, such as amp-hours and watt-hours.
- **“Ground is always Earth, zero, and safe.”** Circuit ground may only be a chosen reference. Earth, protective earth, chassis, signal return, and circuit common have different roles.

</section>

<section>

## 9. Failure modes and limits

Voltage becomes a problem when the electric field or resulting current and energy exceed what materials, components, or people can tolerate.

- Excess electric field can cause insulation to undergo electrical breakdown.
- Capacitors and other equipment can retain hazardous stored energy after disconnection.
- Applying voltage outside a component's rating can damage it.
- Parasitic inductance and capacitance during fast switching can create transient voltage spikes.
- A floating, weakly referenced conductor can show an unexpected potential through capacitive coupling.
- Electrical work on or near exposed energized parts requires task-appropriate qualification and workplace safety controls.

</section>

<section>

## 10. Safety scope

This lesson is conceptual. It is not a guide for working on household wiring, switchboards, EV chargers, solar inverters, industrial panels, or energized equipment.

Only people qualified for the specific task should perform electrical work on or near exposed energized parts, using the procedures and protective equipment required by their workplace. This is a general U.S. occupational-safety boundary from CDC/NIOSH, not a definition of who is legally qualified in Thailand; Thai law, employer rules, and site procedures must be checked separately. Turning equipment off or disconnecting a source does not prove that every part is de-energized or free of stored energy.

</section>

## Technical track

The voltage at point `a` relative to point `b` can be written:

`V_ab = V_a - V_b`

This is the “`a` relative to `b`” convention. It is the negative of the change defined for going from `a` to `b`: `V_ab = -(V_b - V_a)`.

For an electrostatic field, the electric-field vector and scalar potential are related by:

`𝐄 = -∇V`

The vector `𝐄` points toward the most rapid decrease of potential under the positive-test-charge convention. Between two points in an electrostatic, conservative field:

`V_a - V_b = -∫_b^a 𝐄 · d𝐥`

This path-independent scalar-potential relation is not generally valid for a nonconservative electric field induced by changing magnetic flux.

In lumped circuit theory, ideal wires are often treated as equipotential nodes. This is an approximation: real interconnections have resistance, inductance, and capacitance. When those effects are not negligible for the circuit being studied, the interconnection needs a more detailed model.

</section>
